# segmentectomy vs lobectomy in stage 1a1 lung cancer

- related: [[lung mass and cancer]]
- tags: #literature #pulmonology
---

<!-- when to perform segmentectomy instead of lobectomy b:209949723603-->

The results of two large controlled trials have shown ==equivalent 5-year relapse-free survival== in individuals with ==T1aN0M0== lung cancer treated with surgical resection via ==segmentectomy== or ==lobectomy== (choice C is correct).

The extent of surgical resection for small, early stage lung cancers has been a matter of debate. In 1995, investigators in a controlled trial of lobectomy vs sublobar resection for tumors with a diameter of 3 cm or smaller without lymph node metastasis found a ==higher rate of local relapse== and a nonstatistically significant but ==lower overall survival== in those undergoing ==sublobar resection==. This led to ==lobectomy being the standard of care== for many years. Over time, increased use of CT imaging, improvements in the quality of CT imaging, and the implementation of CT imaging-based lung cancer screening led to the ==identification of more small lung cancers== and lung cancers with an indolent behavior (often subsolid in density). Results from observational studies suggested the possibility that subgroups exist who would have ==equivalent cancer treatment outcomes with a sublobar resection== or a lobectomy. These subgroups included individuals with ==subsolid lung nodules== (often adenocarcinoma in situ or minimally invasive adenocarcinoma), ==those older than 70 years==, those with ==complete lymph node sampling==, and those with ==tumors smaller than 2 cm== in diameter.

Two large controlled trials have helped to shape the care of individuals with tumors 2 cm or smaller in diameter. The first was a randomized, controlled noninferiority trial that randomly assigned 1,106 patients from 70 institutions in Japan to receive a lobectomy or segmentectomy. All tumors had to be either solid or, if part-solid, the diameter of the solid component had to be greater than one-half of the total diameter. At 7.3 years of follow-up, the ==5-year overall survival== was ==94.3% for segmentectomy== and ==91.1% for lobectomy==, showing both ==noninferiority and superiority== (choice D is incorrect). Improved overall survival was seen in all subgroups (sex, age ≥70 or <70, smoking status, tumor location, solid or part-solid, adenocarcinoma or not adenocarcinoma). The ==median reduction in FEV1 percent predicted was 3.5% lower in the segmentectomy group== (choice B is incorrect), and the ==frequency of grade 2 or higher postoperative complications was similar== (choice A is incorrect).

The second trial was a multicenter noninferiority trial of lobectomy vs sublobar resection (mostly segmentectomy) in patients with non-small cell lung cancer 2 cm or smaller in diameter who had pathologically confirmed node-negative disease intraoperatively. Six hundred ninety-seven patients were randomly assigned intraoperatively. At a median follow-up of 7 years, the 5-year disease-free survival was 64.1% in the lobectomy arm and 63.6% in the sublobar arm, and the 5-year overall survival was 78.9% in the lobectomy arm and 80.3% in the sublobar arm. Additional information, gained from observational studies, suggests that, even if occult nodal disease is found (approximately 6.1% of those with clinical stage IA disease), there may be no difference in overall survival between segmentectomy and lobectomy (whereas overall survival is lower if a wedge resection is the surgery performed), and survival of those with adenocarcinoma in situ and minimally invasive adenocarcinoma (usually pure ground glass or part-solid with small solid components) managed with wedge resection is equivalent to treatment with lobectomy.[^1]
