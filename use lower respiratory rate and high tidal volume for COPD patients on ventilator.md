# use lower respiratory rate and high tidal volume for COPD patients on ventilator

- related: [[COPD chronic obstructive pulmonary disease]]
- tags: #literature #pulmonology
---

This patient has the classical presentation of an acute exacerbation of COPD (AECOPD), and despite initial use of the best form of ventilatory support, namely bilevel NIV, he has failed this therapy and requires intubation with low respiratory rate to avoid worsening his already-present lung hyperinflation because of the risks of barotrauma and compromise of circulation (choice D is correct).

Multiple studies have demonstrated benefit from use of NIV in the early management of AECOPD, with clear demonstration that NIV reduces intubation rate and mortality. However, this same literature also showed that a fraction of patients will fail NIV, usually in the early hours of management, and attention to rescue of this subset with intubation and invasive support is essential to ensure a good outcome. Trying to adjust the NIV system in a patient with declining mental status and significant increase in degree of hypercapnia would not be appropriate and would risk cardiopulmonary arrest (choice B is incorrect). ==Patients failing NIV do not do so because of loss of hypoxic drive== to breathe but ==because of loss of respiratory muscle reserve==, and expecting them to improve by reducing oxygen administration is unfounded (choice A is incorrect). Intubating and selecting ventilator settings of low tidal volume and relatively high respiratory rate, as would be appropriate for a patient with ARDS, would be unwise in this setting (choice C is incorrect). This patient does not have evidence of acute lung injury but rather has worsened chronic ventilatory failure with worsened airflow obstruction, worsened hyperinflation, and acutely worsened hypercapnia. High respiratory rates, with their associated shorter expiratory times, would be very likely to worsen hyperinflation in this patient, increasing the risk of pneumothorax and hypotension. Instead, a better strategy is to use ==lower respiratory rates== (beginning at 12/min and reducing them even further in some patients) and ==slightly higher tidal volumes==.[^1]
