# 6 min walk test correlates to quality of life in post ARDS survivors

- related: [[ARDS acute respiratory distress syndrome]]
- tags: #literature #pulmonology #icu
---

<!-- which metric correlates with post ARDS quality of life? -->

As the field of critical care medicine has achieved major advances in survival of patients with many disorders, there has been a parallel recognition of persistent morbidity that follows critical illness. This is particularly true of patients surviving ARDS. Diminished quality of life and high levels of health resource usage in ARDS survivors have been shown to correlate to a simple measure of functional capacity, ==the 6-min walk==.

In early longitudinal series studying outcomes after ARDS, a logical focus on pulmonary function demonstrated that in the months after hospital discharge, ==mild restrictive and obstructive abnormalities== were ==often present== with a reduced diffusing capacity, a prominent finding over the ensuing 6 months. However, ==lung function often improved== and ==did not correlate== well with the very persistent reduced quality of life noted as long as 5 years after the index hospitalization.

While acute right heart failure can be seen in a significant fraction of patients with severe ARDS, left ventricular dysfunction is not common absent an independent left heart insult. Most survivors of ARDS do not experience cardiac limitation of their activity. Rather, most physical limitation of activity appears to be related to ==persisting neuromuscular dysfunction==, the common ICU-acquired weakness (ICU-AW) seen in survivors of critical illness. This syndrome encompasses a myopathy and/or polyneuropathy. ICU-AW presumably arises from many insults attendant to ICU care, including bed rest, inflammatory states, malnutrition, treatment with glucocorticoids or neuromuscular blocking agents, and prolonged duration of care. This aspect of physical limitation can be captured by dynamic tests of functional state such as the 6-min walk test.

The effects of ICU-AW that diminish quality of life in ICU survivors are compounded by the common neuropsychiatric complaints that are seen in these patients, including anxiety, depression, and posttraumatic stress syndrome. These conditions are well described in family members, as well as in patients. Recognition of the high prevalence of these disorders has prompted critical care services to coordinate their care with longer-term assessment and treatment teams to optimize the return of patient and family to their prior capacities and quality of life.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6]
