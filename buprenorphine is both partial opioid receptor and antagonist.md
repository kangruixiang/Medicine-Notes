# buprenorphine is both partial opioid receptor and antagonist

- related: [[opioid management inpatient]]
- tags: #literature #icu
---

<!-- how does buprenorphine work b:1286467276182-->

==Buprenorphine== would be an appropriate option for treating this patient's OUD. It is both a ==partial agonist of the mu opioid receptor==, which accounts for its euphoric and analgesic effects, and an ==antagonist of the kappa opioid receptors== (KORs). As a component of the antireward system, KORs are normally stimulated by stress and mediate despair-like responses. By blocking KORs, ==buprenorphine may have antidepressant effects==.

Prior to December 2022, clinicians in the United States were required to obtain a special waiver from the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) before they could prescribe buprenorphine to treat OUD. Now, though, ==any clinician who has a DEA registration== that includes Schedule Ill authority ==can prescribe buprenorphine for OUD==.[^1]

Buprenorphine is available as a ==transmucosal tablet or film== and as an extended-release ==subcutaneous injection==. The transmucosal (eg, sublingual, buccal) format is most common, and it is often prepared in combination with ==naloxone==. The combination discourages the misuse of buprenorphine through dissolving and injecting tablets or film because ==naloxone== has essentially no transmucosal absorption, yet it ==will lead to opioid withdrawal symptoms== if dissolved and injected intravenously.
